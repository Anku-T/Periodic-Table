Classification of Elements

Introduction to Elements

What are elements?

Elements are pure substances made of only one type of atom. For example, gold (Au), oxygen (O₂), and sodium (Na) are elements.

Why classify them?

Scientists noticed that some elements behave similarly (e.g., sodium and potassium both react violently with water). By grouping them, we can:

  • Predict properties of unknown elements.
  • Understand trends in reactivity, atomic size, and bonding.

Early Attempts: Mendeleev’s Periodic Table (1869)

Basis: Atomic weight (average mass of an element’s atoms).

Mendeleev’s Periodic Law:

"The properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic weights."

Key Features/Properties:

  • Arranged elements in rows (periods) and columns (groups).
  • Left gaps for undiscovered elements (e.g., predicted "eka-aluminum," later found as Gallium).
  • Some elements were placed out of order to match properties (e.g., Tellurium before Iodine).

Limitations:

  • Couldn’t explain isotopes (same element, different weights, e.g., C-12, C-14).
  • No clear position for hydrogen (behaves like both metals and non-metals).
  • Failed to Accommodate Noble Gases.

Mendeleev’s Periodic Table

Modern Periodic Table(Henry Moseley, 1913)

Basis: Atomic number (number of protons).

Modern Periodic Law:

"The properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers."

Properties:

  • It has 18 groups and 7 periods.
  • It is based on the atomic number of elements.
  • It accommodates all elements, even undiscovered ones.

Limitations:

  • No Clear Placement for Isotopes.
  • Lanthanides and Actinides Are Separated.
  • Transition Metals Show Irregular Trends.

Electronic Configuration

"The path in which electrons revolve around the nucleus of an atom is called orbit or shell."

Shells and Subshells

A shell can be divided into one or more subshells, which contain orbitals where electrons are found.

Sub-shells can accommodate a maximum number of electrons.

  • s:2 electrons
  • p:6 electrons
  • d:10 electrons
  • f:14 electrons

Aufbau's Principle

Aufbau's principle dictates that electrons fill subshells in order of increasing energy:1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d < 5p < 6s < 4f < 5d < 6p

Classification: Metals, Non-metals, and Metalloids

Elements are classified into three groups based on metallic character.

Metal

  • Located on the left side of the periodic table.
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Alkali metals (Group IA) have one electron in their outermost shell.
  • Alkaline earth metals (Group IIA) have two electrons in their outermost shell.
  • Transition metals (Groups IIIB to IIB) are less reactive and have properties between active metals and non-metals.

Non-Metals

  • Located on the right side of the periodic table.
  • Elements of groups VA, VIA, and VIIA, along with elements of group VIIIA or 18 (0).
  • Halogens (Group VIIA/17) have seven electrons in their valence shell and are very reactive.
  • Noble gases (Group 0) have eight electrons in their valence shell, making them inert.

Metalloids

  • Elements between metals and non-metals.
  • Exhibit properties of both metals and non-metals.
  • Poor/semiconductors of electricity.
  • Examples include Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), and Bismuth (Bi).

Trends in the Periodic Table

Valency

  • The outermost shell of an atom is called its valence shell.
  • The electron present in the valence shell of an atom is called valence electron.
  • Valency changes across a period (IA to VIIA, then Group 0): 1, 2, 3, 4, 3, 2, 1, 0.
  • All elements in a group have the same valency.

Atomic Size

  • Atomic Size Atomic size decreases from left to right across a period due to increasing nuclear charge.
  • Atomic size increases from top to bottom within a group due to the addition of electron shells.

Electropositivity and Electronegativity

  • Electropositivity: The property of an element to lose valence electrons and form positive ions (cations).
  • Electronegativity: The property of an element to gain electrons in its valence shell to form negative ions (anions).
  • Electropositivity (metallic character) decreases from left to right across a period, while electronegativity (nonmetallic character) increases.
  • Electropositivity increases and electronegativity decreases down a group.

Chemical Reactivity

  • The chemical reactivity of metals decreases across a period, while the chemical reactivity of non-metals increases.
  • The chemical reactivity of metals increases down a group, whereas the reactivity of non-metals decreases.
